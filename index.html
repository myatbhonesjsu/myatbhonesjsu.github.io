myatbhonesan In June 2024, Universal, Sony, and Warner sued AI music companies Suno and Udio — alleging their models were trained on decades of copyrighted recordings without permission or payment. But these lawsuits are about much more than one legal dispute. They sit at the intersection of four unresolved tensions that scholars across law, technology, and music studies have been debating for years.
Swipe through this carousel for a breakdown of each tension, grounded in current research. Here's the short version 👇
01 — Attribution. AI models are "black boxes" that train on thousands of artists simultaneously. Sankaran (2026) calls this an "attribution crisis" — when training data is hidden, the link between creativity, credit, and compensation breaks down entirely. Lee and Lee (2025) show this also destabilises existing copyright management systems built around traceable ownership.
02 — Creativity. Yasuda and Maruyama (2025) argue that AI doesn't create — it statistically recombines. Without emotion, lived experience, or cultural intention, can the output qualify as original art? Zhang and Kim (2025) found that even musicians who use AI tools worry it erodes the emotional authenticity that gives music its cultural value.
03 — Economics. Campos-Valverde and Kaye (2025) find that cheap AI-generated music risks flooding the market, making it harder for independent artists to earn a living. Proposed licensing solutions — where AI firms pay for training data access like streaming royalties — may still favour large corporations over individual creators.
04 — Copyright. Samuelson (2023) identifies the central legal question: does training on copyrighted material transform those works into something new, or simply reproduce them differently? U.S. copyright was built around human authorship. Lim (2023) argues fair use doctrine may adapt — but only if courts demand transparency about what AI was actually trained on.
The Suno and Udio cases don't just ask who owns a song. They ask what authorship, originality, and fair use mean in an age where the composer is invisible, the training data is hidden, and the "artist" has never lived a single human moment.
